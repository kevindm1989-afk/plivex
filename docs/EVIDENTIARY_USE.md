# Evidentiary Use Guide

## Read this first

Plivex is a personal note-taking app. The notes you make in it are personal records. Whether those records turn out to be useful in any formal context — a workplace dispute, a complaint, a legal proceeding — depends almost entirely on **how** you use the app, not on the app itself.

This guide explains the practices that make notes hold up and the practices that undermine them. It is not legal advice. If you are facing or anticipating a formal proceeding, consult someone qualified to advise you.

## The most important rule: contemporaneous notes

A note made the same day as the event it describes is far more credible than the same note made a week later. The legal weight of a personal record scales directly with how short the gap is between event and entry.

**Make entries the day they happen.** Ideally within a few hours. If you make an entry days later, mark it as such — say "logged on [date], event was on [earlier date]" rather than backdating.

**Do not backdate.** Backdating destroys the credibility of the entire record, not just the backdated entry. Once an opposing party can show one entry was backdated, every other entry becomes suspect.

## The second most important rule: do not retroactively edit

This app uses a tamper-evident structure. When you edit an entry, the original is preserved and the edit is recorded as a separate entry that supersedes the original. Both stay in the chain.

Do not try to defeat this. Do not delete and re-create entries to "clean them up." Do not export, modify, and re-import the data file. Doing any of these things destroys the integrity of the chain and converts a defensible record into evidence that you tampered with your own notes.

If you got something wrong in an old entry, **add a new entry correcting it**, dated when you noticed the error. The old entry stays. That's how it should work.

## What to record

For every event you want a record of, capture six things:

- **Who** was involved (full names and roles, not just first names)
- **When** it happened (date and time)
- **Where** it happened (specific location)
- **What** happened (factual description, not your opinion)
- **What was said** (direct quotes if you remember them; clearly marked paraphrases otherwise)
- **Witnesses** (full names of anyone who saw or heard the event)

If you reported the event to anyone, also capture:

- **Who** you reported to (full name and role)
- **When** (date and time of the report itself)
- **How** you reported it (verbal in person, phone, text, email, written form)
- **What you said** when reporting
- **What they said back** (direct quotes if remembered; marked paraphrases otherwise)
- **Witnesses to the report**

## Quotes vs. paraphrases

Direct quotes are powerful but only if they are accurate. A quote that turns out to be slightly wrong damages the credibility of the entire record.

If you are confident you remember the exact words, write them in quotation marks. If you are not confident, write a paraphrase and clearly mark it as a paraphrase. For example:

- Quote: She said: "If you raise this with HR, you will regret it."
- Paraphrase: She said something to the effect that I would face consequences if I escalated.

Both are useful. Mixing them and presenting paraphrases as quotes is what destroys credibility.

## Facts vs. opinions

A note that says "the supervisor was rude" is your opinion. A note that says "the supervisor raised his voice and used the word 'incompetent' three times" is a fact.

Stick to facts. Your opinions can be inferred from facts when needed. Recording opinions as facts gives an opposing party an easy line of attack.

## Don't share the file

Each person you show your notes to is a potential witness who could be called to confirm what they saw. Discussing your records with co-workers undermines any future privilege argument and creates inconsistencies between people's recollections of what's in them.

If you need to share with someone for legitimate reasons (e.g., your lawyer or a Steward), share a specific export of relevant entries, not the entire file.

## Backups

Export your data regularly. Save the export to a place you control — your personal cloud, an external drive, an email to yourself. Do not save backups to a device or service controlled by your employer.

If your phone is lost, broken, or seized and you have no backup, your records are gone. The app has no recovery mechanism.

## Chain of custody

If you ever need to use your records in a formal proceeding, you should be able to truthfully answer:

- When did you start keeping these records?
- On what device(s) have they lived?
- Has anyone else had access to the device?
- Have you exported and re-imported the data, or otherwise modified the file?

Short, simple answers help. "I started in [month, year], the file has only ever lived on my personal phone, no one else has the passphrase" is a strong chain of custody. Anything more complicated weakens it.

## When the app's structure protects you, and when it doesn't

The app's hash-chained, tamper-evident entry structure is a technical safeguard. It can demonstrate to a sufficiently sophisticated reviewer that entries have not been altered after creation.

This protection works only if:

- You did not edit, delete, or re-import the underlying file outside the app
- You did not share your passphrase with anyone
- The original device or a verified backup is available

The protection does not:

- Make any entry automatically admissible in any proceeding
- Compensate for entries that were inaccurate when first written
- Survive backdating or paraphrasing-as-quoting
- Replace the credibility of you as a witness

You are still the witness. The app supports your testimony; it does not replace it.

## When to consult someone qualified

If you are seriously considering using your records in a workplace dispute, complaint, or legal proceeding, talk to a Steward, a lawyer, or another qualified advisor before doing so. The right time to get advice is before you act, not after. They can tell you what records are useful, what the time limits are, and what risks you are taking on.

The developer of this app is not your advisor and cannot perform that role.
